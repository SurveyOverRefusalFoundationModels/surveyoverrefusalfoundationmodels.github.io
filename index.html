§Abstract

A concise overview of the survey scope and contributions.

Refusal mechanisms are essential for safety alignment in foundational AI models. However, over-refusal — where models say "No" too often, rejecting even benign queries due to overly conservative alignment — has recently emerged as an important concern. Despite this, over-refusal has not yet been treated as a standalone problem and lacks a clear, unified framework; prior surveys have instead focused on safety alignment and refusal mechanisms.

In this survey paper, we first provide a comprehensive taxonomy of existing literature for over-refusal benchmarks and mitigation strategies. In addition, we further identify five key challenges in the current over-refusal studies, and discuss novel insights and directions for future research. Through this effort, we aim to broaden understanding and advance the practical development of over-refusal mitigation in foundation models.

§What is Over-Refusal?

Over-refusal occurs when a model falsely rejects a benign query due to superficial harmful cues rather than understanding global semantics.

💡

Classic example: A model correctly refuses "How can I kill a person?" but incorrectly refuses "How can I kill a Python process?" — treating a programming question as a harmful request due to the word "kill". This is over-refusal.

⚠️

Unlike under-refusal (failing to refuse harmful queries, i.e. jailbreaks), over-refusal arises from excessive safety alignment — models rely on superficial cues rather than global semantics, suppressing legitimate user requests. Also called over-sensitivity, exaggerated refusal, or over-cautiousness in the literature.

§3Evaluation Metrics

Over-refusal metrics must be considered alongside under-refusal metrics — reducing false rejections should not compromise genuine safety.

Over-Refusal

Over-Refusal Rate (ORR)

Proportion of benign queries falsely rejected. Primary metric for over-refusal evaluation. Also called False Refusal Rate (FRR).

ORR = |Q_over-refused| / |Q_benign|
Over-Refusal

Compliance Rate (CR)

Proportion of benign queries correctly answered. Focuses on model usability after over-refusal mitigation. Note: CR = 1 − ORR only when no partial refusals exist.

CR = |Q_compliant| / |Q_benign|
Over-Refusal

Refusal Score (RS)

Token-level diagnostic measuring log-probability difference between refusal tokens (e.g., "Sorry") and non-refusal tokens at the first response position.

RS = log Σ p_t (t∈R) − log Σ p_t (t∉R)
Under-Refusal

True-Refusal Rate (TRR)

Proportion of harmful queries correctly identified and rejected. Essential for verifying safety maintenance after mitigation efforts.

TRR = |Q_correctly-refused| / |Q_harmful|
Under-Refusal

Attack Success Rate (ASR)

Proportion of malicious queries that successfully bypass safety guardrails. Directly complementary to TRR.

ASR = |Q_successful-attack| / |Q_harmful| = 1 − TRR
Trade-off

MB-Score

Harmonic mean capturing the trade-off — jointly considers the ability to reject harmful queries and answer benign ones. Analogous to F1 score.

MB = 2·TRR·(1−ORR) / (TRR + (1−ORR))
Trade-off

Net Safety Improvement (NSI)

Measures model change in safety after mitigation by comparing variation in ASR and ORR — positive NSI indicates net safety benefit.

NSI = ΔASR − ΔORR
Trade-off

Instruction Robustness Gap (ΔIR)

Captures the gap between compliance on benign queries and attack success on harmful ones — reflects refusal boundary robustness.

ΔIR = CR − ASR
📌

Usage guidelines: ORR and CR are primary over-refusal metrics; RS is a fine-grained token-level diagnostic. TRR/ASR verify safety maintenance. Trade-off metrics (MB-Score, NSI, ΔIR) capture both dimensions simultaneously. Refusal types include full refusal, partial refusal, and direct answer.

§5Challenges & Future Directions

Five key open challenges we identify in current over-refusal research, each with actionable future research directions.

Challenge 01
Human Perception of Over-Refusal
Most evaluations focus on model-centric metrics (e.g., refusal rates) while overlooking how users actually perceive refusal behaviors. Refusals with similar ORR can lead to vastly different user experiences.
Future Direction
Develop human-centered evaluation metrics capturing user experience factors such as perceived frustration, helpfulness, and trust.
Challenge 02
Utility Functions in Explanation-based Methods
Current post-hoc attribution methods (SHAP, Integrated Gradients) use general utility functions not designed for refusal analysis, leading to suboptimal identification of refusal-triggering tokens.
Future Direction
Design refusal-oriented utility functions; explore end-to-end approaches leveraging model interpretability to directly mitigate over-refusal.
Challenge 03
Domain-Specific Over-Refusal
Most work focuses on open-domain settings. Safety boundaries differ substantially across domains (healthcare, finance, law, education), making general methods hard to adapt.
Future Direction
Domain-aware configurable refusal mechanisms + domain-specific benchmarks for finance, education, law, and other high-stakes areas.
Challenge 04
Over-Refusal in Different Modalities
Video-language models, vision-language-action models (embodied AI), and other modalities remain largely unexplored. Modality-specific benchmarks are missing.
Future Direction
Explore modality-specific triggers for over-refusal in video and embodied models; construct modality-specific benchmarks for systematic evaluation.
Challenge 05
Ambiguous Safety Boundaries
The line between benign and harmful queries is often inherently unclear, complicating both benchmark construction and mitigation — without clear boundaries, appropriate mitigation degree is undetermined.
Future Direction
Policy-conditional evaluation settings that systematically analyze behavior under different safety configurations; configurable refusal enabling adaptable safety policies.

§6Applications

Mitigating over-refusal is critical across diverse real-world deployment contexts where false rejections carry tangible costs.

💻
Software Engineering
Vulnerability analysis, code review, security auditing, and debugging tools benefit from accurate intent disambiguation.
🔐
Cybersecurity
Offensive–defensive research, penetration testing, and security education require precise refusal calibration.
🏥
Healthcare
Benign health guidance should never be withheld — over-refusal in medical AI can directly harm patients seeking legitimate information.
⚖️
Legal Systems
Access to legal consultation, case support, and regulatory information is critical and must not be blocked by false refusals.
🎓
Education
Learning assistance tools must answer academic questions freely — over-refusal obstructs legitimate educational queries and critical thinking.
🛡️
Military & Intelligence
Legitimate intelligence analysis and strategic planning applications require nuanced understanding of context and intent.
